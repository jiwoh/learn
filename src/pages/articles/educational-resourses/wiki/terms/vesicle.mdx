## Definition
    
    A vesicle is a small spherical cell organelle, typically ranging from 0.1 to 0.2 micrometers in size. It is bounded by a membrane, and contains a variety of materials including proteins, enzymes, lipids, sugars, and nucleotides. Vesicles are found in both eukaryotic and prokaryotic cells.
    
    ## Significance in Mycology
    
    Vesicles play an important role in a variety of fungal processes. They are involved in the transport of essential metabolites, the secretion of enzymes, and the uptake of nutrients. Vesicles are also involved in the synthesis of other cellular components and the formation of spores. Additionally, within the hyphae, vesicles are important for regulating cell shape and growth. As a result, vesicles are essential for the proper functioning of mycelia.

# Usage

Vesicles are a type of organelle commonly found in the cells of fungi, and can play a significant role in mycological research. Vesicles store and transport essential nutrients, enzymes, and other cellular components, thus helping to facilitate metabolic processes and ensure the health of the organism. Vesicles can also be used to study the transport of exopolysaccharides and other molecules across the cell membrane, providing insight into important cell processes. Additionally, vesicles have been observed to be involved in the process of fungal growth, allowing researchers to better understand how the structure and composition of fungi change over time. Finally, vesicles can be used to study the effects of environmental factors upon fungal growth and development.
