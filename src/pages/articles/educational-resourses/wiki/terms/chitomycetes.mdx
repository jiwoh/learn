# Chitomycetes
    Chitomycetes is a class of terrestrial and some freshwater fungi, formally known as Chytridiomycota, in the Eumycota kingdom. The Chitomycetes class is unique among fungi due to its unique features, such as the presence of a wall around its nucleus, chitinous cell walls, and the presence of motile, zoospore cells.
    
    ## Significance
    Chitomycetes are important to mycology, as they are the only class of fungi that have evolved the ability to produce flagellated zoospores, which can actively search and infect other hosts. They also play an important role in nutrient cycling in terrestrial ecosystems. Additionally, they are important as they are used in biotechnology as a source of enzymes and agents for biocontrol of plant and animal pests.

Chitomycetes are widely used in mycology to identify fungal species. Due to their diverse morphological characteristics, they are especially valuable in taxonomical studies. For example, the presence or absence of appendages, such as chitin shells, septa and ascomata, in addition to the different shapes and sizes of the spores, are useful to accurately distinguish species within a genus, species complex or phylogenetic group. Chitomycetes are also used in various other studies, such as detecting a species' resistance to various chemicals or to determine its pathogenic potential. Furthermore, knowledge of fungal species can be used to optimize the production of certain metabolites, such as enzymes, pharmaceuticals, and food additives.
