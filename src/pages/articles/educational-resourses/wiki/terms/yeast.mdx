# Definition of Yeast 
    Yeast is a type of single-celled fungus which produces spores. Yeast cells are known for their ability to ferment sugars, which is a process that involves breaking down complex carbohydrates into simpler forms of sugar such as glucose and alcohol. 
    
    ## Significance of Yeast to Mycology
    Yeast is an important organism for the study of mycology due to its wide range of metabolic pathways and its ability to produce alcohol and carbon dioxide as byproducts of fermentation. Scientists and researchers studying mycology are able to use yeast as a model organism to study the effects and implications of various metabolic pathways by observing how the pathways and byproducts of fermentation are affected by external conditions or interventions. The study of yeast has also led to numerous advances in biotechnology, such as the production of beer, bread, and other products.

# Usage

&lt;p&gt;Yeast is a species of unicellular fungi that is widely used in mycology research and practical applications. Yeast is often used as a model organism to study the biology of other eukaryotic organisms, including humans. This is due to its relatively simple genetic makeup and its ability to quickly adapt to new environments. For example, yeast has been used to investigate the regulation of gene expression and the functional aspects of various genes, as well as the development of gene therapy and drug delivery systems. Additionally, yeast is also widely used in industry as a source of enzymes for food production and as a starter material for brewing and fermentation processes. Thus, yeast is a widely used and important organism in mycology research, as well as in industrial applications.&lt;/p&gt;
